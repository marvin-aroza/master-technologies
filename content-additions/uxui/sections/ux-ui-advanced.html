UX Fundamentals & Gestalt Principles

Understanding human psychology to create intuitive, frictionless interfaces.

The Gestalt Principles of Perception

Gestalt psychology explains how the human brain automatically organizes visual elements into groups or unified wholes.

  • Proximity: Elements placed close to each other are perceived as related. This is the most powerful grouping principle. Use negative space (whitespace) to group logical sections.
  • Similarity: Elements that look alike (color, shape, size) are perceived as having the same function. If all your primary buttons are blue, users will click a blue banner expecting an action.
  • Continuity: The eye is compelled to move through one object and continue to another. Used heavily in carousels and horizontal scrolling lists to guide the user's vision off-screen to indicate more content.
  • Closure: The brain prefers complete shapes. If you show an incomplete icon or a clipped image, the brain fills in the missing visual gaps automatically.
Fitts's Law & Hick's Law
  • Fitts's Law: The time required to rapidly move to a target area is a function of the distance to the target and the size of the target. Translation: Make primary action buttons large and place them close to where the user's cursor/thumb already is. Edges and corners of screens are "infinitely" deep targets for a mouse.
  • Hick's Law: The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices. Translation: Don't put 15 links in a dropdown. Group choices into a hierarchy, or hide advanced options behind a "More" button to reduce cognitive load.

Micro-Interactions & Component Design

The tiny details that separate merely functional apps from premium, delightful experiences.

Designing State

A button isn't just a rectangle; it is a state machine. Every interactive component must account for at least 5 states:

  • Default: The baseline appearance.
  • Hover: Visual feedback that the element is interactive (cursor changes, slight shadow, color shift). Note: Hover does not exist on mobile.
  • Focus: Critical for keyboard accessibility. Must have a high-contrast outline indicating this is the active keyboard target.
  • Active / Pressed: Physical feedback that the click is registering (a physical push-down effect or ripple).
  • Disabled: Indicates the action is currently unachievable, usually via low opacity and a `not-allowed` cursor. Pro UX tip: Avoid disabled buttons if possible; instead, leave them enabled and show a helpful error explaining *why* it failed when clicked.
Color Theory & Contrast
  • The 60-30-10 Rule: A classic design rule. 60% of the UI should be the dominant color (usually white/dark gray background), 30% a secondary color (cards, sidebars), and 10% an accent color (primary buttons, active links).
  • WCAG Contrast Ratio: Text must have a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 against its background for normal text (WCAG AA). Never use medium-gray text on a light-gray background, it is physically inaccessible to millions.
  • Meaningful Color: Never use color alone to convey meaning. Colorblind users cannot differentiate a red error state from a green success state easily—always include a supporting icon (✅ or ❌).
